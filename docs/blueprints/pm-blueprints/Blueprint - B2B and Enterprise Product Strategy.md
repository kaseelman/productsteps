# Blueprint: B2B & Enterprise Product Strategy

> Extended Competency 24 | Source: Lenny's Newsletter & Podcast

## What This Skill Means

B2B and enterprise product strategy is the discipline of building products that are purchased through organizational buying processes rather than individual consumer decisions. It means understanding that your "user" and your "buyer" are often different people, that purchasing decisions involve procurement teams, security reviews, and multi-stakeholder sign-offs, and that the path from first touch to signed contract can take months or years. A PM with this skill can navigate the tension between building for the end user who loves the product and satisfying the enterprise buyer who signs the check.

This competency encompasses identifying your ideal customer profile (ICP), designing products that can expand from individual adoption to team and company-wide deployment, aligning product development with sales cycles, managing feature requests from large accounts without losing product vision, and building pricing and packaging that supports land-and-expand motion. It also means understanding when and how to move upmarket -- from SMBs to mid-market to enterprise -- and the organizational changes that transition demands.

At the highest level, B2B product strategy means you can construct a go-to-market motion that matches your product's complexity and price point to the right customer segment, build a sales pitch rooted in differentiated positioning rather than feature walkthroughs, and design a product experience that generates product-qualified leads for your sales team while also serving self-serve customers. You understand that B2B product-market fit is not binary but a continual process of finding fit with progressively larger market segments.

## Why It Matters in 2026

In 2026, the B2B landscape has been transformed by AI. Enterprise buyers are simultaneously more sophisticated in their evaluation processes and more overwhelmed by the proliferation of AI-powered tools competing for budget. The result is that "no decision" -- the buyer's inability to confidently choose -- has become an even larger competitor than any named rival. PMs who cannot help their sales teams overcome buyer indecision through clear positioning and differentiated value will watch deals stall indefinitely.

At the same time, the PLG revolution has raised the bar for what enterprise buyers expect: they want to try before they buy, they expect seamless onboarding, and they have zero patience for products that require months of implementation before delivering value. The most successful B2B PMs in 2026 are building products that work beautifully for individual users on day one while also meeting the governance, compliance, and integration requirements that enterprise procurement teams demand. This dual mandate -- delightful individual experience plus enterprise-grade infrastructure -- is the defining challenge of modern B2B product strategy.
