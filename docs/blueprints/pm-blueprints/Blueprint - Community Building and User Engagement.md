# Blueprint: Community Building & User Engagement

> Extended Competency 20 | Source: Lenny's Newsletter & Podcast

## What This Skill Means

Community Building and User Engagement is the discipline of creating spaces and programs where your users create value for each other -- transforming a one-directional product-customer relationship into a self-reinforcing network of mutual benefit. It is the difference between building an audience (where you help people) and building a community (where you help people help each other). This subtle but massive distinction in mindset is what separates products that retain users through utility alone from those that retain users through belonging, identity, and social ties.

For product managers, this skill involves understanding when to invest in community (and when not to), designing community programs aligned to specific business objectives (support, product feedback, acquisition, contribution, engagement, or success), and building the operational infrastructure that allows community to scale without proportional headcount growth. It means recognizing that communities are not marketing channels -- they are organizational structures for value creation that require dedicated strategy, measurement, and sustained investment.

This skill also encompasses the broader concept of ecosystem building: leveraging partners, creators, advocates, and integrations that already have access and trust with your target audience. In a world where traditional growth channels (inbound, outbound, product virality) are becoming saturated, ecosystem and community strategies are emerging as the most resilient and differentiated growth engines.

## Why It Matters in 2026

The convergence of several forces makes community and ecosystem skills essential in 2026. First, AI has dramatically lowered the cost of content creation, flooding every channel with derivative material and eroding trust in brand-generated content. Users increasingly turn to voices they already trust -- community members, creators, and peers -- making these intermediaries more valuable than ever. Second, traditional growth channels are deteriorating: search traffic is declining due to LLMs, outbound inboxes are overflowing with AI-generated messages, and the explosion of new AI-built products has made virality harder to achieve.

Companies that crack the ecosystem code are seeing extraordinary results. ElevenLabs hit $100M ARR with just 50 employees, powered by a paid creator ecosystem. Supabase grew from 1 million to 4.5 million developers in under a year, largely through integration partners and open source community. Lovable's Discord community surpassed 100,000 members, differentiating it in the crowded vibe-coding space. The question is no longer whether to build an ecosystem strategy, but how to design one that creates a genuine flywheel.
