# Blueprint: Product Vision & Roadmapping

> Domain: Product Strategy | Competency 8 of 12

## What This Competency Means

Product Vision & Roadmapping is the craft of defining an inspiring multi-year trajectory for your product and then translating that trajectory into a ruthlessly prioritized sequence of bets. Vision answers "Where are we going and why does it matter?" Roadmapping answers "What do we build next, and in what order, to unlock the most value along the way?"

This competency is not about producing a Gantt chart or a feature list on a timeline. It is about creating a narrative so compelling that engineers, designers, executives, and customers can see themselves in a future that does not yet exist -- and then making hard sequencing decisions that sacrifice good ideas in service of great ones. The best product visions are not detailed blueprints; they are painted pictures of a world transformed by the product's existence, specific enough to guide decisions but flexible enough to absorb new information.

Roadmapping, in turn, is the practice of disciplined saying-no. Every feature on a roadmap represents dozens of features that did not make the cut. The PM who excels here understands that the sequence in which you build things matters as much as what you build. A feature shipped too early wastes resources; a feature shipped too late concedes the market. The roadmap is a living argument about where the highest-leverage work lies right now.

## Why It Matters in 2026

The pace of change in 2026 demands that product teams hold vision and adaptability in tension simultaneously. AI is reshaping entire product categories in months, not years. As Paul Adams (Intercom CPO) described, the launch of ChatGPT was a "before and after moment" that caused Intercom to rip up their strategy and rebuild from first principles overnight. Teams that cling to rigid roadmaps will be disrupted; teams without a vision will chase every shiny object.

The companies winning in 2026 -- Shopify with CEO-set yearly themes, Miro with their three-year painted picture, Stripe with real product strategies that make planning take three days instead of six weeks -- all share a pattern: strong directional vision paired with lightweight, adaptable planning processes. The annual planning ritual that consumes six weeks and is abandoned by March is a relic. The future belongs to PMs who can articulate a clear strategy and let that strategy do the heavy lifting of prioritization.
