# Blueprint: Go-to-Market Strategy

> Extended Competency 14 | Source: Lenny's Newsletter & Podcast

## What This Skill Means

Go-to-market (GTM) strategy is the plan for how a product reaches its target customers, establishes its position in the market, and builds a sustainable engine for acquiring and expanding accounts. It encompasses market entry decisions (which segment to target first), positioning (how customers perceive your product relative to alternatives), distribution channels (how customers discover and buy your product), launch strategy (how to generate initial traction), and the ongoing evolution of these choices as the company scales.

GTM is where product strategy meets commercial reality. A product manager who understands GTM can answer critical questions that determine a company's trajectory: Should we start with SMBs or enterprises? Should we be product-led or sales-led? What is our positioning relative to incumbents and alternatives? How do we get our first 1,000 customers? How do we expand from one segment to the next without losing what made us successful in the first place?

The best GTM thinkers understand that these choices are deeply interdependent. Your market segment determines your pricing, which determines your distribution model, which determines your product requirements. Getting the sequence right -- and knowing when to evolve each decision -- is what separates companies that achieve product-market fit from those that wander.

## Why It Matters in 2026

The GTM landscape in 2026 is being reshaped by several forces. Product-led growth has matured from a buzzword into a well-understood playbook, but the data consistently shows that even the most successful PLG companies eventually add sales teams. AI tools are making it possible for smaller teams to execute sophisticated go-to-market motions that previously required large organizations. And the proliferation of vertical SaaS and niche products means that positioning -- the art of defining what category you compete in and why you win -- has never been more important.

Meanwhile, the lines between B2B and B2C are blurring. Enterprise products increasingly need consumer-grade onboarding. Consumer products are finding enterprise revenue. PMs who understand the full spectrum of GTM motions, from bottom-up viral adoption to top-down enterprise sales, will be able to guide their products through the inevitable transitions that growth demands.
