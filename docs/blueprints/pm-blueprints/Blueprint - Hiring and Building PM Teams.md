# Blueprint: Hiring & Building PM Teams

> Extended Competency 25 | Source: Lenny's Newsletter & Podcast

## What This Skill Means

Hiring and building PM teams is the discipline of recruiting, evaluating, onboarding, and developing product managers who can deliver outsized impact. It goes beyond writing job descriptions and conducting interviews -- it means understanding what skills actually predict PM success at your specific company, designing interview processes that surface real signal rather than performance, building a team culture that attracts top talent, and creating career development structures that retain your best people. As a PM leader, this may be the single highest-leverage activity you perform, because the quality of your PMs determines the quality of every product decision downstream.

This competency encompasses defining what "great" looks like for a PM at your company (which varies dramatically across organizations), building interview processes that reliably identify those qualities, creating onboarding programs that get new PMs to full productivity quickly, designing career ladders that clarify expectations at each level, and fostering a PM culture of learning, mentorship, and continuous growth. It also means knowing when to hire your first PM, when to promote from within versus hiring externally, and how to build diverse teams that bring different perspectives to product decisions.

At its most advanced, this skill means you can build a PM organization from scratch -- defining the role, the career ladder, the interview process, the team structure, and the cultural norms -- in a way that consistently produces high-performing product teams and becomes a recruiting advantage in itself.

## Why It Matters in 2026

In 2026, the PM role is being redefined by AI. The foundational PM skills -- leadership, execution, strategy, communication -- remain essential, but the specific competencies within each have shifted. PMs now need fluency with AI tools, the ability to evaluate and iterate on AI-powered features, and comfort with a dramatically faster pace of iteration. Hiring processes that were calibrated for the pre-AI world may be selecting for the wrong attributes.

At the same time, the supply of aspiring PMs has never been higher, while the demand for truly excellent PMs remains intense. The challenge is not finding candidates -- it is reliably distinguishing between those who can talk about product management and those who can actually do it. Companies like Linear, Automattic, and PostHog have pioneered work trials that provide dramatically more signal than traditional interviews, and these approaches are becoming the new standard for organizations serious about hiring quality. The PM leaders who thrive in 2026 are those who treat their hiring process with the same rigor they apply to their product -- continuously testing, measuring, and iterating.
