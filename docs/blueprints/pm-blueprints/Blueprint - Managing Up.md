# Blueprint: Managing Up

> Domain: Influencing People | Competency 12 of 12

## What This Competency Means

Managing up is the discipline of interfacing effectively with senior executive leadership -- your direct manager, their manager, and the broader executive team. It encompasses abstracting complex technical details and granular metrics into strategic narratives that executives can act on. It includes securing budget and headcount by framing resource requests in terms of business outcomes. It involves managing executive exposure to risk by delivering bad news early, clearly, and with a path forward. And it means building the kind of trust that earns you increasing autonomy over time.

Managing up is often misunderstood as political maneuvering or impression management. In reality, it is about creating a productive working relationship with the people who control your team's resources, set your constraints, and evaluate your performance. The best practitioners of this skill treat their manager as a partner, not a gatekeeper. They proactively provide the context their leaders need, translate between the detail level their team operates at and the altitude their executives think at, and make it as easy as possible for their boss to champion them in rooms they are not in.

This competency also includes the art of strategic storytelling: the ability to take a complex product situation -- competing priorities, uncertain data, technical constraints, market shifts -- and distill it into a narrative that is clear, compelling, and actionable for a senior leader who has three minutes of attention and a dozen other problems on their plate.

## Why It Matters in 2026

In 2026, the relationship between PMs and executives has intensified on multiple fronts. Companies are running leaner, which means resource allocation decisions are more competitive and more scrutinized. AI is changing what is possible, which means executives are constantly asking "Why aren't we moving faster?" and "What should we be doing with AI?" PMs who cannot translate technical possibilities and constraints into strategic language will be left out of the conversations that determine their team's future.

The rise of the founder-mode ethos has also created a dynamic where many CEOs are more deeply involved in product decisions than they were during the era of professional management. PMs must learn to work productively with intensely product-minded executives who have strong opinions and want to be involved in the details. This requires knowing when to involve leadership, how to present options rather than decisions, and how to maintain creative autonomy for the team while keeping the executive informed and invested.
