# Playbook: Turning Around a Struggling Product

> Situation Playbook | Source: Lenny's Newsletter & Podcast

## The Situation

The metrics are declining. Leadership is nervous. The team is demoralized. Maybe you were brought in specifically to fix this, or maybe you were already on the team when things started to slip. Either way, you are now the person responsible for a product that is losing users, missing goals, or failing to deliver on its promise. Everyone is watching, and opinions about what went wrong are everywhere.

Turnarounds are among the hardest assignments in product management because they combine strategic complexity with emotional difficulty. You need to diagnose the root cause while simultaneously managing anxious stakeholders, rebuilding a demoralized team, and delivering quick wins that demonstrate momentum. The temptation is to swing between two extremes: either declaring everything broken and proposing a complete overhaul, or making incremental tweaks that avoid confronting the real issues. Neither works. The best turnaround PMs find the third path -- an honest diagnosis paired with a phased plan that delivers early credibility while solving deeper structural problems.

What makes this especially challenging is that a struggling product often has struggling relationships behind it. When things go wrong, trust erodes -- between PM and engineering, between the team and leadership, between the company and its users. Your job is not just to fix the product but to rebuild these relationships. That requires a particular kind of courage: the willingness to acknowledge what is not working, to have difficult conversations, and to take responsibility for the path forward even when the problems were not yours to begin with.

## The Playbook

### Phase 1: Diagnose Honestly (Week 1-3)

1. **Conduct a ruthless assessment of the current state.** Pull the data. What are the actual trends for key metrics -- retention, engagement, revenue, NPS? Separate the narratives from the numbers. Often, the team's story about what is happening does not match what the data shows.
2. **Talk to churned and unhappy users.** The most valuable signal in a turnaround comes from people who left or are about to leave. Set up 10-15 conversations with churned users and ask: What made you leave? What would bring you back? What were you hoping for that you did not get?
3. **Interview the team.** Talk to every engineer, designer, and stakeholder privately. Ask: What do you think is the core problem? What have we tried that did not work, and why? What would you do if you had full authority? You will often find that the team already knows the answer -- they just were not empowered to act on it.
4. **Map the competitive landscape.** Has the market shifted? Have competitors launched something better? Is the category itself declining? Sometimes a product is not struggling because of internal failures but because the external environment changed.
