# Playbook: Working with Founders as a PM

> Situation Playbook | Source: Lenny's Newsletter & Podcast

## The Situation

You are a product manager at a company where the founder (or founders) are deeply involved in the product. Maybe you are the first PM hire at a startup, brought in to add structure to a founder-driven product process. Maybe you are a senior PM at a scaled company where the CEO still has strong opinions about every feature. Either way, you find yourself in one of the most nuanced and politically complex roles in product management: adding value to a product that someone else conceived, built, and feels enormous ownership over.

The fundamental tension of this role is that the PM discipline is built on the premise that the PM drives the product vision, strategy, and roadmap. But when you work with a founder-CEO, they often hold that role -- and they have something you do not: the authority of having created the company, the credibility of having bet their career on the idea, and (frequently) the final say on everything. Your challenge is to make yourself indispensable without stepping on the vision that predates you.

What makes this situation emotionally taxing is the ambiguity of your authority. You may be told you "own" the product, but the founder can override any decision. You may be asked for your recommendation, but the founder may have already decided. You may spend weeks on a strategy that the founder rewrites on a napkin in five minutes. The PMs who thrive in this environment are the ones who reframe the relationship: you are not there to own the vision -- you are there to make the founder's vision real, to bring discipline to their intuition, and to tell them what they need to hear even when it is uncomfortable. That last part is the hardest and the most valuable.

## The Playbook

### Phase 1: Understand the Founder (Month 1)

1. **Study how the founder makes decisions.** Jules Walter's first influence tactic is directly applicable here: seek intel on how each stakeholder makes decisions. What does the founder value most -- data, customer stories, competitive analysis, gut instinct? Who do they consult? What are they afraid of? Keep a notes doc where you write down the questions they ask and the feedback they give in meetings.
2. **Learn the origin story deeply.** Why did the founder start this company? What problem were they personally trying to solve? What early decisions shaped the product's DNA? The more you understand the founder's original insight, the better you can extend it rather than contradict it.
3. **Identify the founder's product superpowers and blind spots.** Every founder has both. Maybe they have extraordinary design taste but ignore operational complexity. Maybe they have deep technical insight but struggle to understand the average user. Your job is to complement their strengths, not compete with them.
4. **Build personal trust before professional trust.** Have conversations that are not about work. Understand what motivates them beyond the company. As Ami Vora describes, the thing that determines how good you are at a job is whether you feel trust with the people around you.
