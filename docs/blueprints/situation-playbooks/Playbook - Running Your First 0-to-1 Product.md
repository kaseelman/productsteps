# Playbook: Running Your First 0-to-1 Product

> Situation Playbook | Source: Lenny's Newsletter & Podcast

## The Situation

You have been handed the greenfield brief: build something new from scratch. Maybe it is a new product line within an established company, or you are the first PM at an early-stage startup. Either way, there is no existing product to optimize, no dashboard to check, no users to talk to. There is just a hypothesis, some ambition, and a blank canvas.

This is simultaneously the most thrilling and most terrifying assignment in product management. The thrill comes from the creative freedom -- you get to define the problem, design the solution, and build something that did not exist before. The terror comes from the absence of guardrails. There is no data to validate your decisions, no established process to follow, and no existing customers to tell you what they want. Every decision is high-stakes because there is no momentum to carry you through mistakes.

What makes 0-to-1 uniquely hard is that the skills that made you successful as a PM on a scaling product -- optimizing funnels, running A/B tests, managing a roadmap -- are largely irrelevant here. The 0-to-1 PM is more like a founder than a traditional product manager. You need strong opinions about what should exist in the world, the resilience to be wrong repeatedly, and the judgment to know when to push through resistance versus when to pivot. Most PMs who fail at 0-to-1 fail not because they lack skill, but because they apply the wrong playbook.

## The Playbook

### Phase 1: Discovery and Conviction (Month 1)

1. **Start with the problem, not the solution.** Use Lenny's problem crystallization framework: What is the problem? How do we know it is real? Who has it? Why is it worth solving now? Write this down in a single page. If you cannot articulate the problem in one sentence, you do not understand it well enough.
2. **Talk to 30+ potential users in 30 days.** This is not optional. Do not build anything until you have deep qualitative evidence that the problem is real, painful, and frequent. As the founders of Gong, Gusto, and Retool showed, obsessive early customer contact is the single biggest predictor of finding product-market fit.
3. **Identify your initial customer persona.** You cannot build for everyone. Pick the narrowest, most specific audience that has the most acute version of the problem. As Lenny's B2B series shows, the journey to PMF starts by getting one company (or one user segment) to love your product.
4. **Develop a strong point of view.** Based on your research, articulate what you believe to be true that most people do not. This is your strategic insight. Without it, you are just building features.
