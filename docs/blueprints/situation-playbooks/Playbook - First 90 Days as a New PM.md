# Playbook: First 90 Days as a New PM

> Situation Playbook | Source: Lenny's Newsletter & Podcast

## The Situation

You just accepted a new PM role. Maybe it is your first PM job, or maybe you are joining a new company after years in the discipline. Either way, you are walking into a room full of people who already have context, relationships, and opinions -- and you have none of these things yet. The stakes feel enormous because first impressions compound. The decisions you make in your first 90 days will determine whether you are seen as a credible partner or an outsider who never quite earned a seat at the table.

What makes this moment so difficult is the paradox at its center: you need to build credibility before you can drive change, but you were hired to drive change. Move too fast and you alienate your team. Move too slowly and people wonder why you were hired. The best PMs navigate this tension by being intensely curious in the early weeks, earning trust through small wins, and only then asserting a point of view on the bigger questions.

The emotional texture of this period is also underappreciated. Impostor syndrome peaks in the first 90 days. You will sit in meetings where acronyms fly past you, where team dynamics you do not yet understand shape every interaction, and where the temptation to pretend you know more than you do is powerful. Resisting that temptation -- and instead leading with questions -- is the single most important thing you can do.

## The Playbook

### Phase 1: Listen and Learn (Week 1-3)

1. **Schedule 1:1s with every key stakeholder.** Engineering leads, designers, data scientists, your manager, peer PMs, and the most tenured IC on your team. Ask each person: What is working? What is broken? What do you wish the PM on this team would do differently?
2. **Map the product's current state.** Use the product yourself obsessively. File bugs. Go through every user flow. Read the last three months of user feedback, support tickets, and NPS comments.
3. **Understand the metrics.** Get access to dashboards. Learn which metrics the team tracks, which ones leadership cares about, and where the gaps are between the two.
4. **Study the strategy documents.** Read the most recent roadmap, strategy doc, OKRs, and any post-mortems. Understand the reasoning behind recent decisions, not just the decisions themselves.
