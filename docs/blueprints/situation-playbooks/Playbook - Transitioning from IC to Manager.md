# Playbook: Transitioning from IC to Manager

> Situation Playbook | Source: Lenny's Newsletter & Podcast

## The Situation

You have been a strong individual contributor PM -- maybe one of the best on your team. You have shipped products, hit goals, and earned the trust of your engineers and designers. Now someone has asked you to manage other PMs. Congratulations. You are about to discover that almost nothing you learned as an IC directly prepares you for this role.

The transition from IC PM to PM manager is one of the most disorienting career shifts in product management. As Lenny writes, "In one sense managing PMs is the same job as an IC PM -- marshaling the resources of your team to ship product and deliver business impact. In practice, it is completely different." Instead of working directly on the product, everything now happens indirectly, through your PM team. The habits that made you successful as an IC -- deep customer knowledge, hands-on roadmap ownership, being the person with all the answers -- can actually become liabilities if you do not let go of them.

What makes this transition emotionally difficult is the loss of direct craft. As an IC, you got daily satisfaction from writing a sharp strategy doc, shipping a feature, or uncovering a customer insight. As a manager, your wins are slower, less visible, and mediated through others. Many new managers describe feeling useless for the first few months -- attending meetings but not "doing" anything. This is normal. The most common mistake is to cope with this discomfort by continuing to do IC work, which simultaneously starves your reports of autonomy and prevents you from developing the new skills you actually need.

## The Playbook

### Phase 1: Shift Your Mindset (Month 1)

1. **Accept that this is a fundamentally different role.** Lenny's essay on IC to manager makes this point emphatically: the transition is not a promotion into a bigger version of the same job. It is a career change. The skills are different, the daily work is different, and the definition of success is different.
2. **Internalize the leverage equation.** Borrowing from Andy Grove: "A PM manager's output = the output of their team + the output of the neighboring teams under their influence." Your job is no longer to produce great work directly, but to multiply the quality and impact of the work produced by others.
3. **Stop doing IC work.** This is the hardest step. Do not write PRDs for your reports. Do not take over their design reviews. Do not attend their standups. If you find yourself doing IC work, ask: am I doing this because it needs to be done, or because it makes me feel productive?
4. **Set up 1:1s with each of your direct reports.** Understand their goals, strengths, challenges, and working style. Ask them what kind of support they need from you. Listen more than you talk.
